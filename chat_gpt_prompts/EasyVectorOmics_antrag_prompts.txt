  - Transcriptomics research is a versatile tool to answer important biological questions. E.g. to elucidate livestock microbiome response to different diets, or investigate the early and late genetic response, and associated molecular functions, involved in better wound healing under different "Wundversorgung" (German, please translate) conditions, or to infer tissue specific genetic responses to stress in synthetic organisms. However, we still lack tools to properly compare gene expression between related genes and between tissues in a gene family context, to do time series, and especially to analyze microbiome gene expression. Tensor Omics projects multidimensional gene expression into a semantic vector space to use simple geometric tools like e.g. distances, angles, and rotations to solve those questions.      

In this, gene expression is normalized for robustness, collected per tissue, and plotted into a gene expression vector space. Gene Families are analyzed separately, and the ancestral conserved expression is identified as a mean expression vector (centroid). Differences (Euclidean distances) are measured from a family's centroid to diverged genes. Next, leveraging a robust and simple empirical approach significant outliers are detected. The directions (difference vectors) in which these outliers diverged from the ancestral function can be leveraged to precisely answer important life science questions about changes in tissue preferences and tissue versatility, find species specific adaptations, elucidate expressional response for biological processes like photosynthesis, or identify which micriobiota respond to what type of diet in which way.  

The project aims at developing the core tensor (vector and matrix fields) analysis tools in a robust, tested and versatile R package with a high performance Fortran backend. An browser based workbench will enable the interactive exploration of the data and results, toggling axes, filtering data points and assigning color and shape based on e.g. molecular function annotation. Tensor Omics has been devised in the Cardamine hirsuta genome project, where it provided evidence for several key insights. Currently, in the context of a funded project we are reproducing scientific results about Drosophila gene expression regarding diets. The framework will be applied to published datasets in order to reproduce biological knowledge and proof Tensor Omics' applicability. Tensor Omics has three work phases and respective goals that partially overlap. First, the existing prototype will be implemented in R with all vector space calculations exported to Fortran for maximum efficiency. The development includes the workbench which will enable a versatile and interactive exploration of the multi-omics vector space with projections, zooming, axes toggling, filtering, etc. Importantly, plot state will be persistable for publication issues. In the second phase, the base of reproduced biological knowledge will be extended on public high quality multi-dimensional transcriptome datasets. These comprise sex driven genes in four animal species and root apex sections covering different stress conditions and species. The reproduction of known scientific results will proof the applicability of Tensor Omics. In the third phase, we will conduct transcriptomics experiments in collaboration within our university and apply Tensor Omics to biological questions that with standard tooling cannot yet or have been hard to answer.  

Concretely, phase one will implement Tensor Omics functions to load and normalize multi-dimensional transciptomis data. The data will be stored in high performance Fortran structures, for which a simple and robust serialization scheme, dubbed "txdata" files, will be developed to enable export and versioning to ensure reproducibility and implement FAIR data principles. This file format will also enable the interchange between high performance computing (R and Fortran) and the workbench (Browser based). The mentionded data normalization will scale the data per gene by standard deviation to correct for inequal axes scales, use quantile normalization to correct for "structural effects" (cite here), and log transform the data to correct for heteroscedacity and outliers.
