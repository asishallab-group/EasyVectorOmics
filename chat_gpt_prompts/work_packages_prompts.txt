Work package 2: Tensor Omics implementation

- We will develop a high performance and robust R package leveraging Fortran. 
- This is done on the basis of a prototype produced in the EasyVectorOmics project and for interactive visualization the published Gene-Expression-Plotter browser based tool. 
- All mathematical definitions of the below implemented methods are documented in the prototype (https://github.com/asishallab-group/EasyVectorOmics)
- The package will be implemented in such a way, that the essential Fortran functions can be compiled to WebAssembly and thus support the interactive exploration and hypothesis building in the workbench
- All functions will be unit tested
- We implement a binary serialization of the data (txdata) for versioning, export, and reproducibility (FAIR data)

  - Normalization of gene expression data (TPM): Gene-Wise scaling by division with standard deviation (no centering) to ensure an isotropic space , quantile normalization to correct for structural effects and ensure comparability, log transformation to correct for heteroscedacity and suppress outliers.

  - Next, funtions are implemented that compute family centroids, either by using the mean vector of a family's orthologs, or the mean family's expression.   - Empirical distributions are established for outlier detection.
    - Relative Distance Index (RDI) enables the identification of outliers using the normalized intra-family from centroid distances. Importantly for downstream analysis the original distance is used.
    - Relative Angle Index (RAI) enables the detection of dosage effects. This empirical distribution is used to identify gene pairs whose direction is significantly similar, by identifying the 5percentile of angles.
    - Relative Axis Signal (RAS) enables the detection of neofunctionalization by identifying genes whose expression is in one axis (e.g. tissue) is in the noise range, i.e. the 5percentile. If paralog copies gain expression in tissues formerly not expressed, they are considered to have undergone neofunctionalization.
    - Rank-normalized Signal Index (RSI) enables the identification of noise expression vectors and following regions of significant expression, isolated by no expression. RSI is calculated as the relative expression in the 95 minus the 5 quartile range. Importatnly, two RSI distributions are constructed, one for all expression vectors and another for all shift vectors, i.e. the changes in expression. 
    - Relative Frobenius Index (RFI) is used to identify subfields of significant signal strength. Subfields are sets of expression or shift vectors. Their aggregated effect in terms of signal strength is estimated by the frobenius norm of the matrix comprising all vecors. This norm is normalized by division with the squareroot of the number of vectors, so that a subfields signal strength is estimated as signal per contained vector. Thus e.g. subfields of vectors known to be involved in photosynthesis can be queried for a significant contribution to expression change e.g. under drought stress.
  - Outlier Detection and Shift Vector Calculation: Shift vectors are generated by subtracting the family centroid from an expression vector. Outliers' normalized length falls into the 95% of the RDI empirical distribution. Outliers are collected and can be queried for molecular function, species, or used to identify sub-, neofunctionalization or dosage effect.
  - Phenotypic data like healing rates and cellular proliferation, livestock weightgain and food-intake, are added as semantic axes to the omics vector space. Normalization uses only centering by division by standard-deviation. For transcriptomics analyses these axes are ignored (toggled off) and are only considered in the context of direction based expression shift detection or gradient analyses. 
  - At this stage the omcis space is fully constructed and consists of the expression field, potentially including phenotypic axes, and the shift field (changes in expression) (figure 1.A). All data is stored in highly performant fortran arrays; meta-data tables are represented by column vectors and indexed using FortranContainer Hashmaps; importantly efficient searches of neighbors in Euclidean or spherical (angle) space are made possible using K-D trees (Bentley-1975). A robust and performant serialization function enables the export of binary data to Tensor Omics data files (txdata format). Those will enable versioning, dissemination, and support reproducibility and comply with FAIR data principles.
