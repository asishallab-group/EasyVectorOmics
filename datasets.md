We have worked with two different datasets: one for insects and another for mammals.

# Dataset 1 Description

## Study Title:
**Interspecies Comparative Analyses Reveal Distinct Carbohydrate-Responsive Systems among Drosophila Species**

## Summary:
The study explores how different Drosophila species respond to diets with varying protein to carbohydrate ratios. The researchers examined larval growth, natural food composition, and gene expression in five species. They found that generalist species adapt to a broad range of nutritional environments, while specialist species show more constrained responses. The study also revealed species specific differences in transcriptional regulation and metabolic pathways related to carbohydrate processing.

The RNA-sequencing data from the study "Interspecies Comparative Analyses Reveal Distinct Carbohydrate-Responsive Systems among Drosophila Species" have been deposited in the DDBJ Sequence Read Archive. The accession numbers for these datasets are:

- DRA004295
- DRA006831
- DRA007810

The corresponding BioProject accession number is PRJDB4481. 

- Link to Paper: [https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2211124719310642](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2211124719310642)

RNA-sequencing was performed on either an Illumina HiSeq 2500 or an Illumina NextSeq 500 using single end reads. 

---

## The Dataset we used

In our study, we included RNA-seq data from three Drosophila species D. melanogaster (generalist), D. simulans
(intermediate dietary flexibility), and D. sechellia (specialist). Data were collected across two tissues. muscle and
whole-body and two dietary treatments: M (medium-composition) and P (protein-enriched). The C (carbohydrateenriched) diet was excluded due to inconsistent data availability. Each condition has three biological replicates.

# Dataset 2 Description

## Study Title:
**Evolutionary convergence of sex-biased gene expression in mammals**

## Summary:
Sex differences in gene expression are widespread and are often modeled in other mammalian species. This study investigates how these differences are conserved across various tissues and species. The goal was to determine the extent to which molecular sex differences are conserved across tissues and species. For this purpose, a survey was conducted, encompassing RNA sequencing data from 12 tissues of five mammalian species. Between 128 and 805 genes with conserved sex-biased expression were identified across different tissues. The results suggest that a significant portion of the sex-biased differences in gene expression is due to evolutionary changes in the regulation by sex-biased transcription factors.

- Link to Paper: [https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aaw7317](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aaw7317)
- Link to NCBI Entry: [https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE125483](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE125483)

RNA sequencing of the tissue samples was performed using the **Illumina HiSeq 2500** platform.

---

## Study Design

The study investigated 12 different tissue types to capture sex-specific differences in gene expression:
- **Thyroid**
- **Spleen**
- **Skin**
- **Pituitary**
- **Muscle**
- **Lung**
- **Liver**
- **Heart**
- **Colon**
- **Brain**
- **Adrenal**
- **Testis**

The study includes four different species:
- **Macaca fascicularis** (Cynomolgus Macaque)
- **Canis lupus familiaris** (Dog)
- **Mus musculus** (Mouse)
- **Rattus norvegicus** (Rat)

For each species, three male and three female animals were studied (before the removal of outliers).
